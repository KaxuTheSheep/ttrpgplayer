# Manufacturing & Additive Production

*Additive manufacturing is mature. Controlling the feedstock is controlling production.*

---

## The Shift

By 2100 additive manufacturing — 3D printing in its mature form — has resolved the current limitations of speed, material range, and structural integrity. The result is a manufacturing model that differs fundamentally from the factory-to-distribution pipeline of the 20th and early 21st centuries.

The factory model shifts from producing finished objects to producing feedstock — the raw material inputs that local print nodes consume. Controlling feedstock is controlling manufacturing. Objects are produced where they are needed rather than shipped from centralised facilities, which reduces logistics complexity but creates a different dependency structure.

---

## What Additive Manufacturing Can and Cannot Do

**What it handles well:**
- Complex geometries that conventional machining cannot produce
- On-demand production without minimum batch requirements
- Local production reducing supply chain length for appropriate objects
- Multi-material objects combining properties in a single print
- Structural components where layer bonding is not the stress point

**What it cannot replace:**
- Objects requiring continuous homogeneous material at a molecular level — cutting edges are the primary example
- Large-scale continuous production where batch manufacturing remains more economical
- Objects whose material properties depend on specific crystalline structures that printing disrupts

**The blade edge problem specifically:** additive manufacturing produces objects with a granular layer structure — each deposited layer is a boundary. A cutting edge requires molecular continuity across its entire length. Printing produces the blade body and rough geometry correctly. The edge itself requires a secondary finishing process — grinding, lapping, or precision edge deposition — applied after printing. Manufacturing is two-stage: print the form, finish the edge. Feedstock control covers the first stage; edge finishing capability is a separate and distinct chokepoint.

---

## The Feedstock Model

The critical shift from present-day manufacturing: the factory does not produce objects. It produces feedstock — the raw material inputs in the forms that print nodes consume. Graphene composite filament. Ceramic powder. Polymer matrices. Metal alloys in printable form. Biological feedstocks for medical applications.

**Why this matters for faction structure:** controlling feedstock supply is equivalent to controlling manufacturing capability. A faction with a print node but no feedstock produces nothing. A faction with feedstock but no print node cannot convert it to objects. Both are necessary; both are potential chokepoints.

Conglomerates that position themselves in the feedstock supply chain have leverage over every faction that depends on printed goods — which in 2100 is most of them for most everyday objects. This is a quieter but more pervasive form of control than owning the finished goods themselves.

---

## Design Files as Commodity

A print node and feedstock are necessary but not sufficient. The third requirement is the design file — the specification that tells the print node what to produce.

Design files are controlled, traded, stolen, and forged. A verified design file for a specific augment component, a particular weapon configuration, or a critical infrastructure part is worth real money to factions that cannot access it through legitimate channels. Conglomerates file proprietary designs and actively pursue unauthorised printing of their specifications.

**The black market dimension:** design files for weapons, augment components, and restricted hardware circulate through the same channels as other contraband. A faction with a print node, appropriate feedstock, and the right design file can produce almost anything — with quality dependent on feedstock quality and print node capability. Grey market designs may be incomplete, outdated, or intentionally degraded. The provenance of a design file is as uncertain as the provenance of black market biological components.

---

## What Is Still Factory-Produced

Not everything migrates to local additive production:

**Synthetic food at city-feeding scale** — Clarent's manufacturing facilities operate on continuous production processes that additive manufacturing cannot replicate economically. The chemistry of synthetic food production is not a printing problem.

**Vehicle frames and major structural elements** — large-scale structural components benefit from continuous casting and forming processes that produce better material properties than printing at that scale.

**Advanced augment components** — the highest-tier MPU configurations, NI limiters, and precision electronic components require manufacturing processes more controlled than current-generation additive production. These are still produced in specialised facilities by conglomerates with the infrastructure to maintain the required precision.

**Feedstock itself** — the raw material inputs for print nodes are produced industrially. This is where the real manufacturing infrastructure concentrates.

---

## District Texture

**Conglomerate and wealthy districts:** print nodes are building infrastructure — appliances as mundane as a refrigerator, producing everyday objects on demand. Current-generation nodes with access to verified feedstock and licensed design files. The supply chain is invisible because it works.

**Mid-tier districts:** print nodes exist as commercial services — district print shops where residents bring design files and pay per print. Feedstock quality is generally reliable. Design file access is the limiting factor — licensed files for common objects, grey market for anything restricted.

**Forgotten districts:** older-generation print nodes, often maintained by local technicians who keep them running past their designed lifespan. Feedstock from uncertain sources — may be off-spec, may be counterfeit, may be repurposed material from other applications. Grey market design files dominate. Output quality is unpredictable. A faction that controls a functioning print node in a forgotten district has a significant local asset.
