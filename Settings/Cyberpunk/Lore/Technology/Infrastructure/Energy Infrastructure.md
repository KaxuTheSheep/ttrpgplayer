# Energy Infrastructure

*A patchwork of generation sources. Reliable where money maintains it. Absent where it doesn't.*

---

## The Generation Mix

New Halcyon's power does not come from a single source. It comes from a layered mix of generation technologies at different scales, with reliability and quality tracking conglomerate territory and investment in the same pattern as every other service in the city.

### Fusion

Viable by 2100 but recently viable rather than mature and universally deployed. The physics of fusion has been understood for decades — it is an engineering problem, and 75 years is sufficient runway for engineering solutions to reach commercial operation. Several large fusion plants operate within the Federal States, owned and operated by conglomerates with the capital to make the initial investment.

Fusion has not had time to displace existing infrastructure comprehensively. It powers conglomerate core facilities and the most valuable districts. Everywhere else is still running on what was already there.

### Fission

Already at scale today. The question for 2100 is maintenance history rather than technology. Fission plants that have been in continuous operation under conglomerate ownership with proper maintenance and capital investment are functional and well-managed. Plants in transitional areas — where ownership has changed hands multiple times, where the maintenance contract is disputed, where the original operating company no longer exists — are ageing infrastructure that nobody has adequately funded.

A fission plant in conglomerate core territory is fine. One in a transitional zone is a background anxiety that residents and nearby factions are aware of and that nobody with the authority to address it has the incentive to prioritise.

### Solar Coating

Building-integrated photovoltaic coating — not panels bolted onto existing structures but a photovoltaic material applied as a surface treatment, integrated into the building material itself. Tesla's solar slate is the present-day precursor; by 2100 the technology is mature enough that photovoltaic coating is a standard option in new construction and renovation.

**Coverage and generation:** a fully coated building generates power from every surface — roof, walls, even road surfaces in principle — though vertical surfaces produce less than horizontal ones at most latitudes due to sun angle. Total generation from a fully coated building is meaningful but not sufficient for complete energy independence under full operational load. Sufficient to substantially reduce grid dependency, maintain essential systems during outages, and power the building management AI continuously regardless of grid status.

**Conglomerate buildings** with self-healing concrete and building management systems almost certainly have photovoltaic coating as standard — it reduces long-term operational cost and provides energy resilience that suits their self-sufficient building philosophy.

**Forgotten district buildings** may have older photovoltaic coating from a previous era — degraded from years without maintenance, generating some power unpredictably from infrastructure nobody is actively managing. Not reliable but not zero.

**Degradation:** photovoltaic efficiency drops as the coating ages and accumulates damage. Self-healing concrete maintains the structural substrate but the coating is a separate material with its own lifespan. Wealthy buildings have coating renewal on maintenance schedules. Forgotten district buildings do not.

## The Grid

A primary grid exists covering New Halcyon in principle — owned by a conglomerate or consortium whose identity and ownership structure is pending design. In wealthy and middle districts it is reliable, maintained, and the infrastructure is current-generation. In forgotten districts the grid connection exists on paper, the physical infrastructure is degraded, and actual reliable power comes from whatever local solutions residents have built.

Grid quality is one of the most legible infrastructure markers in the city — not because the presence or absence of electricity is dramatically different at a glance, but because what the electricity enables differs. Building management systems, wireless charging infrastructure, terahertz coverage nodes, and consistent appliance power all require stable, clean power at sufficient capacity. Forgotten districts with degraded grid connections have power that fluctuates, cuts out, and cannot support the infrastructure layers that depend on it.

---

## The Co-Dependency Problem

Power, network, and building management infrastructure are operationally co-dependent. A power disruption in a district:

- Cuts local terahertz wireless coverage as antenna nodes lose power
- Degrades or eliminates vacuum optical trunk tap points if they lack backup power
- Brings down building management systems that do not have independent generation
- Eliminates wireless charging infrastructure for framework batteries

Three infrastructure systems failing together rather than independently. In wealthy districts backup power and redundant infrastructure prevent this cascade — conglomerate buildings with photovoltaic coating and battery storage can island themselves from the grid and maintain full operation. In forgotten districts the cascade happens regularly and no contractual obligation exists to restore it on any particular timeline.

## The Political Dimension

Energy independence versus energy dependency maps onto the FS-UT cold war in a specific way.

Hydrogen as a fuel — viable by 2100 and preferable to electric for high-energy-density applications — is cheapest when produced from renewable electricity through electrolysis. The UT's West Coast renewable infrastructure makes it a natural hydrogen producer. A Federal States that shifted to hydrogen dependency would layer an energy dependency on top of the existing food dependency and the biological component dependency.

FS conglomerates that built electric charging infrastructure actively prefer the status quo. Their investment is in domestically controlled charging networks. Hydrogen infrastructure would require either sourcing from the UT or building new domestic renewable generation at scale — both options that reduce the value of existing conglomerate infrastructure positions.

This is not a conspiracy. It is market incentives producing a geopolitically convenient outcome that nobody needed to coordinate.
