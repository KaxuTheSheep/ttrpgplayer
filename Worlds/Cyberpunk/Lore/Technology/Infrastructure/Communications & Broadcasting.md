# Communications & Broadcasting

*Content moves through conglomerate-owned infrastructure. Coverage is the editorial decision nobody has to make explicitly.*

---

## The Distribution Model

Broadcasting in 2100 is not a single transmitter pushing signal to passive receivers. It is content distributed through the same [[Network Infrastructure|network infrastructure]] as everything else — conglomerate-controlled nodes pushing to whoever is connected within their coverage area.

This collapses the distinction between infrastructure control and editorial control. A conglomerate that owns the local network nodes decides what bandwidth gets allocated to what content in their territory. Blocking a broadcast is not a legal or political act requiring justification — it is a network management decision, the same category as any other traffic prioritisation. The absence of content in a district can be produced without anyone making an explicit decision to censor. The bandwidth simply was not allocated.

## Coverage as Class

**Wealthy and conglomerate districts:** high bandwidth, dense [[Network Infrastructure|terahertz coverage]], full fidelity content delivery. Rich media, real-time, personalised — the content experience is tailored to the individual based on consumption history and, for paired users, biometric state. A building leader agent that knows the executive is stressed does not recommend agitating content.

**Middle districts:** same content at lower fidelity with more latency. Compression artefacts in video, audio quality reduced, real-time interactive content delayed enough to degrade the experience. The content is nominally the same. The experience is noticeably different.

**Forgotten districts:** whatever leaks through from trunk infrastructure passing beneath them. Primarily text and low-resolution audio at the bandwidth available from degraded coverage. Rich media is technically accessible but practically unusable at the speeds available. Residents in forgotten districts are not informationally isolated — they have access to the same nominal content — but the friction of access is high enough that consumption patterns differ substantially from wealthier districts.

## Personal Display Technology

Nobody carries a flat glass rectangle in 2100. Personal display technology has moved in two directions depending on augmentation status.

**For augmented individuals:** display is delivered directly to augmented eyes — content appears in the visual field without any external device. Communication, media consumption, and information display happen in the user's perception rather than on a screen they are looking at. The distinction between the world and displayed content is a rendering choice rather than a physical one.

**For unaugmented individuals and contexts where eye augments are unavailable:** flexible display surfaces worn on the wrist or forearm. The display material itself is fabric-weight — thin enough to fold when not in use, conforming to the body when worn, unrolling flat when a display surface is needed. Not a rigid screen. A surface that happens to display.

Public terminals exist in wealthy and middle districts for transactions, navigation, and access to services that require a physical interface. They are absent or non-functional in forgotten districts — another infrastructure gap that accumulates friction for residents who lack personal display capability.

## Payment and Transaction Texture

Payment friction is a class signal as legible as any other.

**Wealthy augmented:** transactions complete through eye movement and confirmed intention — a look, a thought, a payment made. The friction is so low it barely registers as an [[Actions|action]]. Augmented users with full financial system integration do not experience purchasing as a distinct activity.

**Middle tier:** gestural or ocular confirmation, slightly more deliberate, occasionally requiring a second factor. Noticeable but minimal.

**Poor districts and unaugmented:** physical payment — tap, card, or physical scrip. Each transaction requires a distinct physical interaction. For those using closed-community currencies or conglomerate scrip rather than Reserve currency, additional friction from currency exchange or the limited acceptance of alternative currencies.

The accumulation of transaction friction across a day is a lived experience of class position that numbers do not fully capture.

## Pirate Broadcasting

For unaugmented people or those without network connectivity, physical-layer broadcast still exists. Low-power transmitters pushing signal through radio frequencies that cheap passive receivers can pick up — no network connection required, no infrastructure dependency beyond the transmitter and receiver hardware.

Forgotten districts have pirate broadcasters operating in this space — community information, local news, music, faction communications encoded in innocuous-sounding content. The physical infrastructure these operate on is separate from conglomerate-controlled network nodes. Signal propagates through air, not through managed fibre or terahertz nodes.

These operations are technically illegal under whatever passes for communications regulation in the Federal States. In practice they are tolerated — they operate on frequencies and at power levels that make them low priority for enforcement, they serve as pressure valves for communities that would otherwise have no information infrastructure, and shutting them down would require physical presence in forgotten districts that serves no clear conglomerate interest.

A pirate broadcaster in a forgotten district is community infrastructure. The person running it knows this and so do the residents who listen.

## The Kessler Context

Satellite communications — historically a significant component of global broadcasting and telecommunications infrastructure — are degraded by 2100 due to orbital debris accumulation. Certain orbital altitudes have become increasingly risky, and maintaining satellite infrastructure requires placing assets in less optimal orbits or accepting higher replacement rates.

This is not a communications collapse — it is a gradual degradation that has made ground-based infrastructure more important than it would otherwise be. The vacuum optical trunk network and terahertz local coverage are not just technologically preferable alternatives to satellite infrastructure; they are partially necessary because satellite reliability has declined. The inequality in ground infrastructure coverage therefore matters more than it would in a world with robust satellite backup.

Country B, with centralised state resources and a strategic interest in maintaining communications capability, is better positioned to sustain functional satellite infrastructure than the fragmented Federal States. This asymmetry in satellite capability is a quiet military and intelligence advantage that neither side discusses publicly.
